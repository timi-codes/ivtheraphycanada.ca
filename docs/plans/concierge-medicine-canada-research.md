North America holds ~38–43% of global market = **USD $8–10B in 2025.**

### Canada Market Size
No standalone Canadian figure exists in any published research. Based on Canada's ~10–12% share of North American healthcare spending, estimated at **USD $500M–$1B in 2025** — but this is early-stage and growing rapidly.

### Growth Drivers in Canada
- 5.9 million Canadians without a regular doctor
- 71% dissatisfied with provincial healthcare (Angus Reid 2026)
- Aging population
- Post-pandemic wellness consciousness
- **Alberta Bill 11** (December 2025) — landmark legislation allowing dual practice

### How Many Clinics Exist
Approximately **30–60 dedicated concierge/executive health programs** across Canada — heavily concentrated in Toronto, Vancouver, Ottawa, Calgary. Alberta alone had **41 identified** in a 2023 review. **No centralized national directory exists.**

### Wait Times for Specialists (Fraser Institute 2025)

- **Best:** Ontario — 19.2 weeks

- 56% faced barriers accessing specialists

- **22% of the top 1%** highest-income North American adults participate in concierge medicine
- Age: 40–65 skew, affluent seniors growing

## 6. Legal & Regulatory Framework

### Is It Legal?
**Grey zone — depends on how the clinic structures its services.**

The **Canada Health Act (CHA)** prohibits:
- Extra-billing above the provincial rate for insured services
- User charges for insured services

Provinces that permit this face CHT deductions — **$62.2M levied in 2024–25** across BC, QC, NB, SK, AB, NS, ON, NL.

### Three Models Clinics Use

1. **Fully Compliant** — Charge only for genuinely uninsured services (nutrition, lifestyle coaching, diagnostics not provincially covered)
2. **Block Fee / Grey Zone** — Bundle access to a physician with uninsured add-ons via membership fee (Medcan model). Critics argue this buys preferred access to insured care
3. **Potentially Non-Compliant** — Membership fee is mandatory to access any physician services, including OHIP-insured care

### Provincial Rules

**Ontario:** CFMA (2004) prohibits charging for preferred access to OHIP-insured services. Block fee provision was challenged legally and struck down temporarily.

**British Columbia:** Most aggressive enforcement. BC obtained **injunctions against two membership clinics** (2023) — both revised their models. Ongoing active monitoring.

**Alberta:** 41 membership clinics reviewed in 2023. CPSA requires:
- Maintain existing patients for routine care regardless of membership
- 45 days written notice before transitioning to concierge
- Allow patients one additional year to continue with physician

**Alberta Bill 11 (December 18, 2025 — NOW IN FORCE):**
- First province to allow **dual practice** (physicians work in both public + private simultaneously)
- Doctors can charge patients directly for medically necessary procedures while working in the public system
- Significant opening for concierge medicine expansion in Alberta
- Federal funding consequences under review

### January 2025 CHA Services Policy ("Holland Letter")
- Extends no-charging rule to nurse practitioners, pharmacists, and midwives
- Explicitly targets queue-jumping for surgical procedures
- Provinces must report compliance by December 2028
- Tightening of federal oversight over private practice models

### Key Compliance Rule
A physician **cannot simultaneously bill the province AND charge patients** for the same service. Most Canadian concierge clinics:
- Retain OHIP/MSP/AHCIP billing for insured services
- Charge separately only for genuinely uninsured components
- The legal test is whether the membership fee constitutes a "user charge" for access to insured care

**Why US players haven't entered Canada:**
1. CHA makes standard US DPC/concierge model difficult to replicate
2. Lower density of high-income consumers vs. US coastal metros
3. Entrenched provincial health systems
4. Liability + licensure complexity across 10 provinces

### Lessons from the US Trajectory
1. **Employer/corporate channel is the growth engine** — Medcan's 2,000+ corporate accounts shows this is already working in Canada
2. **Physician conversion works** — MDVIP converts practices; doctor reduces panel from 2,500 to 600, charges membership. Possible in Alberta under Bill 11
3. **DPC is different** — US DPC ($50–$150/mo) serves millions of middle-class patients. Canada has no equivalent
4. **US took off post-ACA (2010)** — Canada's inflection could come via provincial deregulation (Alberta) or public system deterioration
5. **Virtual-first concierge** is the fastest US growth segment — lowest regulatory friction in Canada too

## 8. Opportunity Gaps

### Services Most In Demand but Undersupplied

1. **Mid-market concierge (~$100–$200/month)** — Canada is bifurcated: free overwhelmed public care OR $4,000–$12,000/year private. Almost nothing in the $1,200–$3,000/year range outside Medcan's entry tier

2. **Virtual-first concierge memberships** — Low regulatory friction, no geography constraint, minimal overhead. No major Canadian player has built a truly national virtual concierge product

3. **Mental health integration** — Canadian concierge care focuses almost entirely on physical/executive health. Mental health-inclusive memberships are extremely rare

4. **Women's health concierge** — No identified women's health-specific concierge provider in Canada. The US has multiple women's health DPC/concierge brands

5. **Longevity medicine / biohacking tier** — WELL Longevity and Medcan are moving here, but room for specialized longevity clinic chains

6. **Corporate/employer-embedded models** — B2B channel is underpenetrated outside Medcan's network

### Most Underserved Canadian Cities

| City | Why Underserved |
|------|----------------|
| **Quebec City** | High physician shortage, no identified concierge providers |
| **Winnipeg** | No identified concierge providers; Manitoba among worst access metrics |
| **Halifax** | 49-week median specialist wait; no identified concierge providers; 21% without family doctor |
| **Regina / Saskatoon** | Saskatchewan worst combined access score (63%); no identified dedicated providers |
| **Hamilton / Kitchener-Waterloo** | Large ON cities, no identified providers despite Golden Horseshoe location |
| **Victoria** | BC has 17.7% physician shortage; Elysian/VCM serve only Metro Vancouver |
| **Kelowna** | Pandosy Village closed to new members — demand exists, supply is closed |

### The Single Biggest Market Gap

The **absence of an accessible, mid-market, digitally-native concierge product** priced at $150–$300/month filling the space between the overwhelmed public system and the $5,000–$12,000 executive health market. In the US this tier serves millions. **In Canada it essentially does not exist.**

The **second major gap:** A **national directory and discovery platform** for concierge medicine in Canada. There is nothing equivalent to Concierge Medicine Today, MDVIP's physician finder, or DPC Frontier's map. The SEO and content opportunity is significant and largely uncaptured.
